Non-Verbal Communication Isn't Always Inclusive

Let me begin with a question. Imagine someone tells you a joke or shares something shocking. What’s your first reaction? Maybe you laugh out loud, give them a high-five, or open your mouth in surprise.

But what if the person you’re talking to is blind? They won’t be able to see your reaction. They won’t know if you laughed, looked surprised, or didn’t react at all.

That leaves us wondering: Did they like my joke? Did I offend them? Are they upset?

One reason for this confusion could be facial expressions. As blind people, we don’t always show strong expressions while speaking.

Sighted people are used to seeing faces while interacting, so they may not understand how we feel. And we can’t see their expressions either. This creates a communication gap.

The Communication Gap

Non-verbal communication—such as facial expressions, hand gestures, and body language—is one of the biggest challenges between blind and sighted people.

Most conversations depend heavily on visual cues. We often hear phrases like “show, don’t tell,” but that approach does not work for blind people.

I often feel left out in conversations with sighted people. It is difficult to understand their expressions, and I cannot rely on facial expressions to communicate my own emotions in the same way.

Many sighted people also do not realize they may need to communicate more clearly using words instead of gestures.

The Social Impact

This communication gap is one reason many blind people, including myself, struggle to build friendships with sighted people.

Sometimes, people assume we do not understand situations simply because we cannot react visually. Others think we do not express emotions, which is far from the truth.

There have been times when people talked about me in front of me using hand gestures or facial expressions, assuming I would not notice.

But we notice more than people think. We hear changes in tone, sense awkward silences, and recognize discomfort or mockery.

One of my friends shared how people at her workplace sarcastically compliment her while silently laughing or making faces. Blindness does not mean we cannot sense emotions or understand social situations.

Friendships, Relationships, and Exclusion

Social gatherings can feel isolating for blind people. In noisy environments, people often communicate through eye contact, gestures, and expressions that we cannot access.

Most friendships and relationships begin through non-verbal interactions like smiles, eye contact, or body language. Since we cannot see or respond to these signals, we are often unintentionally left out.

Many people see blind individuals as someone to “help” rather than someone to genuinely connect with.

There is also a misconception that love and romance do not exist for blind people. But disability does not remove emotions, feelings, or the need for connection.

Blind people experience emotions, relationships, and love just as deeply as anyone else.

A Gap That Affects Everyone

This communication gap affects sighted people too. Some may want to interact but hesitate because they are unsure how to approach.

Someone might try to wave or make eye contact, expecting a response. When none comes, confusion follows.

Thankfully, there have been people in my life who took the time to understand me and chose to connect despite uncertainty. That effort means everything.

Conclusion

Non-verbal communication creates a major gap between blind and sighted people.

Blind people cannot see visual reactions, while sighted people may not know how to communicate without them.

I simply want sighted people to understand that our way of expressing is different. Please do not rely only on facial expressions or gestures while speaking with us.

Small misunderstandings can turn into large stereotypes, which ultimately isolate and exclude blind people even more from society.
