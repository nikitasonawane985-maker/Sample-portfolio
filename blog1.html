My White Cane, My Companion

Published on World White Cane Day

Today is World White Cane Day, a day that holds a very special meaning for me. My cane is more than just a mobility tool, it’s my constant companion and a symbol of my independence. It helps me move confidently, even in places I’ve never been before.

But my relationship with my cane wasn’t always easy or confident. When I first started using it, I often felt shy or embarrassed. I used to wonder what people might think or how it might look to them, and sometimes I wished I could manage without it. However, over time, my perspective changed.

I’ve realized that people often stare at things they don’t understand, things that look different from what they consider “normal.” But this cane that once made me self-conscious is the very thing that gives me freedom and safety. It allows me to travel independently, explore different places, and live life on my own terms.

Many people feel uncomfortable when they see a person with a white cane. Some may want to help but don’t always know how. So here are some white cane etiquettes everyone should know:

White Cane Etiquette

  • Don’t grab the cane: My cane helps me understand my surroundings, hurdles, bumps, and steps. When someone grabs the cane, it makes it difficult for me to walk, and I could even bump into something or fall if there are steps.
  • Always ask before offering help: If you wish to guide me, just ask, “Would you like some help?” If I agree, let me hold your arm instead of you pulling me.
  • Don’t walk in front of the cane: This can be difficult in a busy place like Mumbai, but the cane helps me identify and navigate obstacles. When someone walks in front of the cane, it can be confusing for me, and even the person in front might get hurt or trip.
  • Use clear verbal directions: Instead of saying “this way” or “over there,” say things like “to your left,” “behind you,” or “straight ahead.”

So today, on World White Cane Day, I celebrate not just my cane, but the journey it represents, from hesitation to acceptance, from fear to freedom.

My cane is not just an object I carry, it’s my constant companion and a part of my personal space.
